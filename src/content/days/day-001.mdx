## The Fear

There's a founder I've been following for two years. He built a company from zero to $10M ARR, writes openly about his journey, and shares lessons that have genuinely shaped how I think about building products.

I've never spoken to him.

Every time I think about reaching out, my brain serves up the same script:

> "He's busy. He gets 500 DMs a day. What makes you special? You don't have anything valuable to say. Wait until you've built something impressive enough to warrant his attention."

That script has been running on loop for 18 months.

## The Action

Today, I opened X, typed his name, and wrote a DM.

I kept it short:

> Hey [name], I've been following your work for the past two years — especially your writing on building in public. I'm starting a 100-day fear challenge and your journey has been a big inspiration. Would love to ask you one question about how you handle rejection as a founder. Got 15 minutes this week?

Six sentences. Thirty seconds to write. A year and a half to send.

## The Outcome

He replied within two hours.

> Sure, happy to help. DM me your calendar link.

That's it. Two minutes of discomfort for a conversation that could change my perspective on building.

## The Reflection

This is the pattern I need to break. The fear isn't in the response — it's in the _composing_. The drafting and deleting. The "what ifs" that play out in my head.

The actual outcome was mundane. A yes. A calendar invite. A conversation that hasn't even happened yet.

But the fear was real. And facing it felt disproportionately rewarding for how small the action was.

## Lesson

**The fear of sending the message is almost always worse than any response you'll get.**

The worst realistic outcome is silence. And silence doesn't hurt — it just is. The best outcome is a door opening.

You miss 100% of the DMs you don't send.

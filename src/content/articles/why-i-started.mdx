## The Gap Between What I Build and Who I Am

I can build a distributed system. I can debug a race condition at 2 AM. I can ship a feature from zero to production in a weekend.

But ask me to record a video of myself talking? My chest tightens.

Send a cold DM to someone I admire? I'll draft it, delete it, draft it again, and close the tab.

Stand up in a room and pitch an idea? I'd rather refactor the entire codebase.

There's a gap between technical confidence and personal confidence. And for a founding engineer, that gap is a liability.

## Why This Matters for AI Startups

The AI startup world moves fast. Really fast. The difference between winning and losing is often just a matter of who's willing to make the uncomfortable move:

- The founder who cold-emailed the first 100 users
- The engineer who recorded the demo video despite hating their voice
- The team that shipped before the product was "ready"

These are all fear-facing actions. And they compound.

A founding engineer who can face rejection, communicate vulnerably, and ship imperfect work is worth more than one who can write perfect code but stays invisible.

## The Jia Jiang Blueprint

[Jia Jiang's "100 Days of Rejection"](https://www.ted.com/talks/jia_jiang_what_i_learned_from_100_days_of_rejection) is the most practical framework for this I've ever seen.

His insight: rejection isn't the problem. The *fear* of rejection is the problem. And the only way to kill the fear is exposure.

He spent 100 days doing things that would get him rejected — asking strangers for absurd favors, knocking on doors, requesting the impossible. And it worked. He desensitized himself to "no."

I'm adapting his framework for my own context: **a founding engineer building in public.**

## My Plan

For 100 days, I'll document:

- **Cold outreach** — DMs, emails, applications
- **Content creation** — videos, posts, articles
- **Vulnerability** — sharing failures, asking for help
- **Networking** — events, conversations, introductions
- **Building in public** — shipping unfinished work, sharing learnings

Every day gets documented here — fear, action, outcome, lesson. No skipping. No hiding.

## What Success Looks Like

If after 100 days I still feel the same fear but I've acted anyway — that's success.

The goal isn't to eliminate fear. It's to build a reflex: when I feel fear, I move toward it instead of away from it.

Because that's what building — really building — requires.
